Inheritance

Just as every individual organism is a descendant of an ancestor that lived before it, so too, every copy of a gene that exists in a population (two copies for every diploid individual) is a descendant of an ancestral gene copy that came before it.

Meiosis and Recombination

Meiosis is the process by which a genome is duplicated and forms gametes. Each chromosome replicates to form two chromatid (thus 4 total copies of every gene). The two homologous chromosomes align and segregate during meiosis I into separate cells. Which copies of each chromosome are joined is random (two equally probable arrangements -- this is independent assortment).

source: https://ib.bioninja.com.au/_Media/random-gene-assortment_med.jpeg

Crossing over

As homologous chromosomes align in synapsis non-sister chromatids may break and *recombine* with their homologous partner (crossing over).

Reproduction and Inheritance

"what is the probability that by chance your parent entirely failed to pass any autosomal DNA from a grandparent to you?" A question of probability.

source: https://gcbias.org/2013/10/20/how-much-of-your-genome-do-you-inherit-from-a-particular-grandparent/

Independent Assortment

Let's first assume no recombination, and focus on a single parent, your mother, and ask how likely it is that she passes on her father's chromosome every single time, and never her mother's. Since there are two copies of every chromosome, each one represents a coin flip, with 50% chance. $$ P(\text{never mom's chrom}) = 1/2 \times 1/2 \times 1/2 ... (\text{22 times}) $$ This can also be written as: $$ P(\text{never mom's chrom}) = 0.5 ^{22} = 2.38 e^{-7} $$ To get the probability that any grandparent fails to pass any DNA to you, you could simply multiply this by 2, since this could happpen either in from your Mom's side or from your Dad's. $$ P(\text{no DNA from a gparent}) = 2 \times 0.5 ^{22} = 4.77 e^{-7} $$

Recombination makes this less likely

With recombination each chromosome inherited from your mother has a greater likelihood of containing DNA from both grandparents, and thus the probability of receiving no DNA from a grandparent is lower. This takes into account the (variable) probability of recombination on different chromosomes, and sexes, in addition to independent assortment. $$ P(\text{no DNA from a gparent | Male}) = 8.8e^{-16} $$ $$ P(\text{no DNA from a gparent | Female}) = 2.8e^{-23} $$

Inheritance patterns

You inherit your mitochondria from your mother's lineage (thus from her mom, and her mom's mom, etc.). Mitochondrial genomes do not recombine. In contrast to autosomal chromosomes which are a mosaic of many ancestral chromosomes, mito genomes are simply copied as a whole. Mutations can occur over time giving rise to new alleles.

Mitochondrial haplogroups

This led to great interest in comparing mitochondrial DNA sequences (haplotypes) to reconstruct evolutionary relationships among populations or species. Animal mtDNA = Approx 16 thousand bp, w/ about 37 genes. All these genes are *highly/perfectly linked*, due to absence of recombination, and thus have the same gene tree history.

source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867415011198

Mitochondrial Eve

In 1987, with mtDNA sequences from approx 150 women, Cann, Stoneking and Wilson published a paper estimating the age (how long ago) the more recent common ancestor of all extant mtDNA genomes existed. Concluding all human mtDNA originated from a single population from Africa, dated to 140,000 -- 200,000 years ago. The individual who represents Eve can change (has changed over time). If some unique descendant lineages were lost it would change to a younger descendant.

source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondrial_Eve

How much of DNA do you inherit from each ancestor?

But let's focus on the 22 autosomes for now, to examine more on how recombination breaks up chromosomes into a mosaic of different ancestors over time.

How much of DNA do you inherit from each ancestor?

As we look at ancestors farther and farther back in time, we find that we have inherited less and less of their DNA. In this case we find an ancestor only 7 generations back that has contributed *NO DNA* to our genome! "Despite being your genealogical ancestor, he is not your genetic ancestor, none of their story has been passed down to you."

Genetic versus genealogical ancestor

  • Each generation back your number of ancestors doubles.
  • Each generation back will halve the amount of autosomal DNA from an ancestor.
  • Only ~9 generations back you are likely to have a genealogical ancestor that is not a genetic ancestor.

Implications: Ancestry Testing

Genetic ancestry tests have gained in popularity across the United States as more Americans seek answers about their ancestral past. The tests have been used to verify or dispute family stories about ancestors or to allow people to seek a sense of belonging with a particular tribe or community. They can also be useful in medical research to identify genetic variants across populations. At the same time, assumptions about genetic testing – and the very idea of a “genetic” identity – pose challenges for communities that are defined in terms of political, social, and cultural identities.

CODIS -- the largest DNA database used by US Law Enforcement, based on 13-20 microsatellite markers (short repeat loci). This database is made up of samples collected by law enforcement through enforced or coerced methods over many years. Although quite large, the amount of information pales in comparison to that available in *private* DNA databases, collected by ancestry companies such as 23andme or ancestryDNA.

Modeling Evolution

  • Evolution: "change in allele frequencies in a population over time"
  • Idealized population: No change in allele frequencies (HW equil.).
  • WF Model: finite population size, allele frequencies change by genetic drift.

Summary of WF exercise

  • A WF model follows the process of random mating in a finite sized population over multiple discrete generations.
  • Interpreting a WF model plot requires understanding the direction of time, and which gene copies are ancestors or descendants of others.
  • The WF process gives rise to genealogical relationships among gene copies.
  • All gene copies in a population will eventually coalesce to a common ancestor.
  • The probability that two gene copies share a common ancestor 1 generation ago is 1 / 2Ne.
  • The expected time until two genen copies share a common ancestor is 2Ne generations.
  • heterozygosity = 2Ne * 2 * mu .
  • For large population sizes WF simulations are kind of slow.

Expectation of genetic diversity

If most of the variation within genomes results from random genetic drift acting on genetic variation with no selective effect on fitness, then we can predict the expected genome-wide heterozygosity as: $$ \pi = 4Ne\mu $$ $$ \pi = 2 \times 2 Ne \times \mu $$

A relationship thus exists between:
PopNe $\leftarrow$ $\rightarrow$ genealogies $\leftarrow$ $\rightarrow$ genetic diversity.

The coalescent

We have a probabilistic (analytical) solution for the expected time until coalescence of two samples. Can we extend this to any arbitrary number (k) of samples? Doing so would allow us to describe the expected genealogy for k sampled gene copies from a population of size N.

The Kingman coalescent

Each additional sample ask: does it share the same ancestor one generation ago: $$ P(\text{no coal}| k, N) = (1 - \frac{1}{2N}) \times (1 - \frac{2}{2N}) \times (1 - \frac{3}{2N}) \times ... (1 - \frac{k}{2N}) $$ The right side of this equation can be multiplied out to simplify its representation as: $$ P(\text{no coal} | k, N) = 1 - [1 + 2 + 3 + ... (k - 1)] \times \frac{1}{2N} + (\text{terms in} \frac{1}{N^2}) $$ Simplify summed term, and discard term on the right (assume only one coal per generation). $$ P(\text{no coal} | k, N) = 1 - \frac{k(k-1)}{2} \times \frac{1}{2N} $$ this simplifies to the more commonly represented form: $$ P(\text{no coal} | k, N) = 1 - \frac{k(k-1)}{4N} $$ Finally, because $P(\text{coal} | k, N)$ = $1 - P(\text{no coal}| k, N)$, we get a solution for the probability that *one* coalescent event occurs in the previous generation given $k$ sampled gene copies in a population with effective size N. $$ P(\text{coal} | k, N) = \frac{k(k-1)}{4N} $$

Kingman coalescent example

We can now model the probability of a coalescent event each generation as a Bernoulli random variable (True or False), with probability: $$ p=\frac{k(k-1)}{4N} $$ This is like a coin flip. We can repeat it many times to ask how many trials will be required, on average, until we observe a heads. This is described by the geometric distribution. The mean of this probability distribution is: $$ E[X] = \frac{1}{p} $$ Thus, the expected time until k samples coalesce once (reducing the number of remaining samples to k-1) is: $$ E[t_k] = \frac{4N}{k(k-1)} $$

Kingman coalescent tree

This is useful because it provides a generative framework to create genealogies as random variables, representing a stochastic outcome of random mating in a population of effective size Ne.

Coalescent variation

If you compare 10 sampled gene copies in a population at 10 different unlinked loci, you would expect to see random genealogical relationships, but predictable waiting times between coalescent events. These waiting times can be used to infer Ne.

Population Structure

Population structure affects the random mating assumption. In a species tree, random maiting only occurs among those in the same partition. Importantly, samples do not always coalesce within a partition, causing incomplete lineage sorting. *two population example*
